Design Thinking
isn't a framework.

Every developer eventually hits the wall: technically correct code, logically sound feature and users still don't get it. Design thinking is the discipline that explains why — and it has nothing to do with aesthetics.

$ git blame user_confusion.txt // most lines: authored by unchallenged assumptions
§ 01

The mental model most devs start with is wrong

The instinct when learning design thinking is to treat it like an API: learn the interface, call the methods, get output. Empathy map → Affinity diagram → How Might We → Prototype. Check, check, check.

That's not what it is. Running the steps without the underlying shift is like copy-pasting code you don't understand. It compiles. It doesn't work.

mental_model.diff what to replace
// Design thinking is... a 5-step process to generate creative solutions a brainstorming technique for product teams a shortcut to validated ideas something designers do, not engineers a discipline for replacing assumptions with observed behaviour a method for deciding what's worth building at all something that makes your code solve the right problem slow by design — speed comes later, after certainty

At its core, design thinking is one repeated act: watching what real people actually do until your assumptions about them start breaking. The frameworks are just scaffolding for that act.

analogy Think of it like debugging a race condition. You don't fix it by reading the code harder. You instrument, observe real execution and wait for the assumption you got wrong to surface. Design thinking is the same discipline applied to human behaviour instead of thread state.

The real output

Most people expect design thinking to output solutions. It doesn't — not directly. Its output is better-formed problems. A well-formed problem is worth 10x more than a solution to a poorly understood one. This is where the ROI actually lives.

§ 02

Four bugs in developer thinking about users

These aren't personality flaws — they're patterns that emerge naturally from how engineers are trained to think. Spotting them in yourself is most of the work.

BUG blame_the_caller()
When many users hit the same error, the instinct is: "they're not reading." But if 60% of users click the wrong button, that's not a user error — it's a signal from the interface.
treat repeated user errors as failed assertions, not user failures
BUG familiarity_blindness()
You built it. Every term on the dashboard is obvious to you. A first-time user sees undocumented symbols in an unfamiliar codebase. Your mental model isn't theirs.
clear_to_me != clear_to_user; always verify with fresh eyes
BUG treat_symptoms()
Adding a tooltip to explain a confusing flow is like adding a catch block that logs and swallows. The error is still happening — you've just hidden the feedback loop.
fix the root cause in the flow, not the surface with explanations
BUG trust_stated_intent()
Users say they want a feature. You build it. Nobody uses it. Intent is self-reported and unreliable. Behaviour under real conditions is the only signal worth trusting.
observed_behaviour.trust() > stated_intent.trust()
the uncomfortable part Fixing these bugs doesn't require new skills — it requires ego flexibility. Each one is a moment where the product is wrong and the user is right, even when it doesn't feel that way.
§ 03

Observation is the only source of truth

The difference between design thinking and product intuition is evidence. Observation is where you collect it — and it's harder than it sounds because the natural instinct is to intervene, explain and help.

the rule During observation sessions: don't correct users. Don't explain the product. Don't finish their sentences. You're not a rubber duck — you're a profiler attached to a production system. Watch, timestamp, note. Intervening corrupts the data.

What you're actually looking for

Individual behaviour is noise. Repeated behaviour across multiple users is signal. A single user pausing on a screen could be anything. Five users pausing at the same point in the same flow — that's a failing assertion worth investigating.

Every "assumption" column above is what a team wrote in their retro before watching users. Every "signal" column is what the observation actually revealed. The gap between those two columns is where bad product decisions live.

A second example: the checkout pattern

Payment step abandonment in a checkout flow. Stack traces show no errors. The initial hypothesis: payment gateway latency.

Observation reveals: users hesitate before selecting a payment method, switch to another app mid-flow, return, then abandon. That's not a latency pattern. That's a trust and total-cost-clarity pattern. Different root cause, entirely different fix. Without watching, you'd have spent weeks optimising the wrong layer.

the shift The question changes from "why aren't users getting this?" to "what are we assuming they already know?" — that second question is the one that leads somewhere.
§ 04

Practising it — no frameworks required yet

Frameworks are useful once the underlying habit is built. Starting with frameworks before the habit is like reaching for a design pattern before you understand the problem you're solving.

Design thinking is learning to be wrong early by watching real people honestly — before you've written the code, not after you've shipped it.

The minimum viable practice

  • [ ] Sit silently during user sessions. Resist fixing. Resist explaining. You're in observe mode, not support mode.
  • [ ] Note pauses, hesitations and abandonment — timestamp them. These are your stack frames.
  • [ ] Look for the same behaviour repeating across at least three different users before treating it as signal.
  • [ ] After each session, ask: what assumption did this break? Write it down before you write any code.
  • [ ] Separate what users say they want from what they do when left alone with the product.
  • [ ] Treat recurring confusion as a failed test — reopen the problem definition, not just the implementation.
expected feeling This will feel unproductive at first. You're watching instead of building. Sitting with confusion instead of resolving it. That discomfort is the signal you're doing it right — you haven't prematurely collapsed the problem space yet.

When frameworks actually help

Once you've got the observation habit — once you can consistently separate what you assumed from what you saw — frameworks like journey mapping and jobs-to-be-done become useful tools rather than cargo cult rituals. They give structure to evidence you've already collected. They don't generate it.
